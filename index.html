The Why

Why does the YCJA exist?

The YCJA recognizes that teens are still developing. Instead of copying the adult system, it creates a separate youth justice system that aims to hold young people accountable in ways that are fair, proportionate, and more likely to help them change.

Different from adults

The youth system is separate because young people have different levels of maturity, stronger support needs, and a better chance of changing their behaviour with the right intervention.

Rehabilitation

The YCJA pushes schools, families, lawyers, judges, and community programs to focus on helping youth learn, repair harm, and avoid future crime.

Reintegration

A major goal is getting youth back into healthy routines like school, work, family support, counseling, and positive community involvement.

The How

What usually happens when a youth gets in trouble?

For less serious, non-violent offences, the law often prefers an out-of-court response first. That can mean a warning, caution, referral, or an extrajudicial sanction instead of pushing a teen straight through the formal court process.

1

Police contact

A youth who is arrested or detained must be told without delay that they have the right to a lawyer.

2

Parent notice

If the youth is detained or formally required to attend court, a parent must be notified as soon as possible.

3

Extrajudicial option

For a first-time non-violent offence, an out-of-court measure may be enough to hold the youth accountable and repair harm.

4

Making things right

The youth may apologize, return stolen property, perform community service, attend counseling, or take part in restorative justice.

Example of an Extrajudicial Measure

Imagine a 16-year-old is caught shoplifting. Instead of a full court process, police or Crown may choose an out-of-court response if it is a non-violent case and the youth takes responsibility.

  • Write an apology or participate in a restorative meeting.
  • Return or pay back what was taken.
  • Complete community service hours.
  • Attend a program about decision-making, theft, or conflict.
Privacy and Records

What happens to a youth criminal record?

The YCJA gives youth more privacy than adults. A youth record is not treated the same as an adult record, and access is restricted to certain people for certain periods of time.

Identity protection

In most situations, the public cannot publish a youth's name or details that would identify them as someone dealt with under the YCJA. That privacy rule helps protect rehabilitation and reduces long-term stigma.

Who can access records?

Access is limited. Police, the court, the youth, the youth's lawyer, parents or guardians, and certain justice officials may access records in specific situations. It is not meant to be open to everyone forever.

Important reality

A youth record does not simply disappear the moment someone turns 18. Access periods depend on the outcome of the case, whether the offence was more or less serious, and whether the person commits another offence before the access period closes.

Bottom line

The YCJA still holds youth accountable, but it tries not to make one mistake define the rest of their life. Privacy and limited records are part of helping a young person reintegrate.

The Impact

Real Toronto-area programs

These are real community-based options that connect youth with restorative justice, accountability, support, and reintegration. Including real local programs strengthens the application part of the assignment.

Toronto Court diversion

Peacebuilders Canada

Peacebuilders runs Restorative Youth Circles for court-involved youth ages 12 to 18. The program focuses on responsibility, making amends, and helping youth move toward a more positive future.

peacebuilders.ca/restorative-justice-programs

Toronto Restorative justice

YNOT Services

YNOT's restorative justice program is geared toward youth and young adults who have been charged with or convicted of criminal or provincial offences. It also includes support for people harmed by youth in the justice system.

ynotservices.org/services/restorative-justice

City of Toronto Community healing

Community Justice Programs

The City of Toronto supports restorative justice programs where youth examine harm, learn conflict-management strategies, and actively participate in solutions that repair relationships and communities.

FAQ

Questions teens actually ask

These answers are written in plain language so you can use them directly in your presentation.

Can I talk to a lawyer if I am arrested?

Yes. Under the YCJA, a young person who is arrested or detained must be told without delay about their right to a lawyer and must be given a chance to contact one. In Ontario, Legal Aid Ontario can help eligible youth.

Do my parents have to be told?

Usually, yes. If a youth is arrested and detained, or if they receive a summons, appearance notice, or undertaking, a parent must be notified as soon as possible.

Will everyone know my name forever?

Usually not. The YCJA normally protects a youth's identity from publication, and youth records have limited access periods instead of staying open like adult records.

Justice Briefing

2 to 5 minute presentation guide

If you need to present this in class, these are the points to hit out loud.

Opening

Explain that the YCJA is for youth ages 12 to 17 and that it is different from the adult justice system because it balances accountability with rehabilitation and reintegration.

Main message

Emphasize that for less serious non-violent offences, the law often prefers extrajudicial measures because they can respond quickly, repair harm, and keep youth from being pushed deeper into the system.

Local connection

Spotlight one Toronto program like Peacebuilders or YNOT and explain how real community supports help youth take responsibility and rebuild trust.
