The Why

Why does the YCJA exist?

The YCJA recognizes that teens are still developing. It separates youth from the adult system and focuses on growth over punishment.

Different from adults

Young people's brains are still developing — especially the parts that control impulse and judgment. The law treats them as people who can still change, not as fully responsible adults.

Rehabilitation first

The YCJA wants youth to understand what they did, fix the harm they caused, and develop the skills to make better choices. Punishment alone is not the goal.

Reintegration into society

A major goal is helping youth return to school, family, and community as quickly as possible without a permanent label that follows them forever.

The How

What usually happens when a youth gets in trouble?

For less serious, non-violent offences the law often uses an out-of-court response first.

1

Police contact

A youth who is arrested or detained must be told immediately that they have the right to contact a lawyer and to have a parent present.

2

Parent notice

If the youth is detained or must appear in court, a parent or guardian must be notified as soon as possible. This is required by law — not optional.

3

Extrajudicial option

For a first-time, non-violent offence, police or Crown may use an out-of-court measure. The YCJA (sections 4 and 6) instructs them to consider this first.

4

Making things right

The youth might apologize, return stolen goods, do community service, attend counseling, or participate in a restorative justice circle with the victim.

5

Court — if needed

For serious offences or if extrajudicial measures are not completed, the case moves to youth court. Sentences must use the least restrictive option that still holds the youth accountable.

Real example: shoplifting at 16

Imagine a 16-year-old gets caught shoplifting $80 worth of goods. It is their first offence and they did not threaten anyone. Police or Crown might use an extrajudicial measure if the youth takes responsibility.

  • Write an apology letter to the store owner.
  • Return or pay back what was taken.
  • Do 10 hours of community service.
  • Attend a program about choices and consequences.
  • Meet with a mediator as part of restorative justice.

If completed, no formal criminal charge is recorded on their file.

Your Rights

Rights youth have under the YCJA

The YCJA gives youth specific legal protections from the moment of arrest. These are guaranteed by law.

⚖️ Section 25

Right to a lawyer

You must be told you can contact a lawyer immediately when arrested or detained. Legal Aid Ontario provides free lawyers for youth who cannot afford one.

👨‍👩‍👦 Section 26

Parent must be told

Police must notify your parent or guardian as soon as possible if you are arrested, detained, or given a court summons. Parents can be present for questioning.

🤐 Charter s.7

Right to silence

You have the right not to answer police questions. Anything you say can be used against you. You should speak to a lawyer before answering any questions.

🔒 Section 110

Identity protection

Your name and any details that identify you cannot normally be published in media or online. This protects you from public stigma while your case is going on.

📋 Section 38

Least restrictive sentence

If you go to court, judges must use the least restrictive sentence that still holds you accountable. Custody is a last resort, not a first option.

🔄 Section 4

Extrajudicial measures

For first-time, non-violent offences, you must be considered for an out-of-court response before being formally charged. Charges are not automatic.

Privacy and Records

What happens to a youth criminal record?

The YCJA gives youth more privacy than adults and only certain people can see a youth record.

Identity protection

In most situations the public cannot publish a youth's name or any details that would identify them. This applies to news articles and social media. Violating this rule is a criminal offence in Canada.

Who can access records?

Police, the court, the youth, their lawyer, parents or guardians, and certain justice workers can see records in specific situations. Employers and the general public generally cannot access them.

When does a record end?

Youth records do not just disappear at 18. The access period depends on the type of offence:

  • Extrajudicial measure: 2 years after it is completed.
  • Summary conviction: 3 years after the sentence ends.
  • Indictable offence: 5 years after the sentence ends.
  • Serious violent offence: may be kept longer or moved to an adult record.

Bottom line

The YCJA still holds youth accountable but tries not to let one mistake define everything. Once the access period ends, most people including employers and the public can no longer see the record. The goal is a real chance at a clean start.

Youth vs Adult

How the youth system is different

The YCJA and the adult Criminal Code treat the same offence very differently.

Myths vs Facts

Common misconceptions about the YCJA

A lot of people misunderstand how youth justice actually works. Here is what is actually true.

Youth can do anything and get away with it

Youth are still held accountable. The difference is how — the system prioritizes learning over punishment, but consequences are real and can include custody for serious offences.

Records can follow you past 18

Depending on the offence, a youth record can remain accessible for up to 5 years after sentencing ends. Serious violent offences may stay even longer or transfer to an adult record.

You automatically go to court if charged

Most first-time, non-violent offences are handled outside court through extrajudicial measures. The YCJA actually instructs police and Crown to consider this option first.

Police can still charge youth with serious crimes

For serious violent offences, youth can receive adult-length sentences even in the youth court system. In rare cases, youth 14 and older can be sentenced as adults.

Your parents can get you out of trouble

Parents are notified and involved, but they cannot override the legal process. Their role is to support the youth, not to speak on their behalf or make the charges disappear.

Restorative justice is a real legal option

Under sections 4 to 12 of the YCJA, restorative approaches like victim-offender mediation and community circles are formally recognized and encouraged as legitimate outcomes.

FAQ

Questions teens actually ask

These answers are plain, honest, and easy to explain in class.

Can I talk to a lawyer if I'm arrested?

Yes. Police must tell you immediately about your right to contact a lawyer. You can call Legal Aid Ontario for free advice even if your family cannot afford one.

Do my parents have to be told?

Usually yes. If you are arrested, detained, or given a summons, a parent or guardian must be notified as soon as possible. They can also be present during any police questioning.

Will everyone know my name forever?

Usually not. The YCJA normally protects your identity from being published. Youth records are also more private than adult ones, and many seal automatically after a period of time.

What if I'm under 12?

The YCJA does not apply to anyone under 12. Children under 12 who break the law are handled under child welfare laws, not the criminal system. They cannot be charged with a criminal offence.

Can I get sent to adult court?

In rare cases, yes. Youth who are 14 or older and charged with a very serious violent offence can receive an adult sentence. A judge decides this — it is not automatic and requires a separate hearing.

What is restorative justice exactly?

It is a process where the youth, the victim, and sometimes community members meet to talk about what happened and agree on how the youth can make things right. It is voluntary and legally recognized under the YCJA.
